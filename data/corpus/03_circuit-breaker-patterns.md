# Circuit Breaker Patterns

## Closed State Fundamentals
The closed state represents normal operation where requests pass through to the downstream service without interference. In this state, the circuit breaker monitors the success or failure rate of requests against configured thresholds. When failures remain below the threshold, all requests proceed normally. This is the default and desired operational state. The circuit breaker tracks metrics continuously during closed state operation, typically counting failures over a rolling time window or request count window. Transitioning out of closed state only occurs when accumulated failures exceed the configured threshold, triggering a move to the open state. Understanding closed state behavior is essential because most of your system's lifetime will spend in this state, and configuration choices made here directly impact when protection mechanisms activate.

## Open State Behavior
The open state activates when failure thresholds are exceeded, and the circuit breaker immediately rejects incoming requests without attempting to contact the downstream service. This prevents cascading failures by failing fast and reducing load on an already-struggling service. Requests in open state typically receive a fail-fast response, either an error exception or a fallback response. The circuit breaker remains in open state for a configured duration called the open timeout or timeout period. During this time, no requests reach the backend, giving it time to recover. It's crucial to set appropriate open state durations—too short and the service may still be recovering, too long and you unnecessarily reject valid requests after the service has recovered.

## Half-Open State Purpose
The half-open state is a transitional state that allows the circuit breaker to test whether a failing service has recovered without fully restoring traffic. After the open timeout expires, the circuit breaker transitions to half-open state instead of returning directly to closed. In half-open state, a limited number of requests (often one) are permitted through to probe the downstream service's health. If these test requests succeed, the circuit breaker assumes recovery and transitions back to closed state, resuming normal operation. If test requests fail, the circuit breaker returns to open state and resets the timeout. This mechanism prevents oscillation between states and provides a gentle way to restore traffic after failures.

## Failure Threshold Configuration
The failure threshold determines how many failures trigger a transition from closed to open state. Thresholds can be configured as absolute counts (e.g., 5 consecutive failures) or percentages (e.g., 50% of the last 100 requests failed). Percentage-based thresholds are generally more robust because they adapt to varying request volumes. Setting thresholds too low causes excessive circuit opening, potentially rejecting requests even when the service remains partially functional. Setting thresholds too high delays protection, allowing cascading failures to propagate further. Effective threshold configuration requires understanding your service's normal error rate, the cost of downstream failures, and acceptable latency when the circuit breaker opens. Thresholds should be based on your specific service characteristics and dependencies.

## Error Budget Integration
An error budget represents the acceptable number or percentage of failures your system can tolerate while maintaining SLO commitments. Circuit breakers protect error budgets by preventing resource exhaustion and cascading failures that consume budget rapidly. When a downstream service fails, continued retry attempts waste resources and burn error budget without recovering the service. By failing fast via circuit breaker opens, you preserve error budget for genuine infrastructure issues rather than wasting it on futile retries. This requires coordinating circuit breaker configuration with error budget calculations—the failure threshold should be low enough to trigger protection before exceeding acceptable error budget consumption. Monitoring both metrics together enables better incident response and capacity planning decisions.

## Bulkhead Isolation with Circuit Breakers
Bulkheads compartmentalize systems to prevent one failure from spreading to all consumers. Circuit breakers enhance bulkhead patterns by providing per-dependency isolation. Rather than a single circuit breaker for all downstream services, implement separate circuit breakers for each critical dependency, ensuring failure in one service doesn't directly impact others. For example, separate circuit breakers for payment service, inventory service, and notification service means payment failures don't prevent inventory checks. This requires tracking metrics and state independently for each breaker. The tradeoff is increased operational complexity—more breakers require more configuration, monitoring, and maintenance. However, the isolation benefits typically outweigh the complexity costs in systems with numerous critical dependencies.

## Fallback Strategies Design
Fallback strategies define what happens when a circuit breaker opens and requests can't reach the downstream service. Common strategies include returning cached responses, serving degraded functionality, providing default values, or returning specific error responses. Effective fallback design requires understanding which operations can degrade gracefully and which cannot. For a user profile service, returning a previously cached profile is reasonable; for payment processing, returning a default value is unacceptable and should return an explicit error instead. Fallback implementation should be local and fast, avoiding secondary calls that might also fail. Testing fallback paths regularly ensures they function correctly under failure conditions, as fallbacks untested in production often contain subtle bugs discovered too late.

## Circuit Breaker vs Timeout Mechanisms
Circuit breakers and timeouts serve different purposes in failure handling. Timeouts limit how long individual requests wait for responses, protecting against slow or hanging connections. Circuit breakers operate at higher level, preventing entire series of requests after repeated failures. Timeouts are reactive—they wait for a response up to the limit. Circuit breakers are proactive—they stop attempting requests after detecting a pattern of failures. Both mechanisms work together: timeouts catch individual slow requests, while circuit breakers prevent sustained request streams to failing services. Setting timeout values shorter than circuit breaker thresholds ensures requests fail fast enough for the circuit breaker to detect problems quickly. Using both together provides defense in depth against various failure modes.

## Concurrent Request Handling in Half-Open
During half-open state, managing concurrent requests requires careful consideration. Most implementations allow only a single request through during half-open state, using others as evidence for state transitions. If that single request succeeds, the service is assumed recovered and the circuit transitions to closed. If it fails, the circuit returns to open. However, some implementations allow a small number of concurrent probes during half-open state to gather more evidence about recovery. Allowing multiple concurrent probes requires careful state management—all probes must succeed to transition to closed state. The tradeoff is between faster recovery detection (multiple probes) and lower load on recovering services (single probe). Single-probe strategies are generally safer for already-stressed services.

## Exponential Backoff in Open State
Exponential backoff adjusts the open timeout duration based on repeated failures, preventing rapid state oscillation. After entering open state, the initial timeout might be 10 seconds. If the service remains unhealthy and the circuit reopens, the next timeout increases to 20 seconds, then 40 seconds, and so on. This prevents overwhelming a recovering service with probe requests and gives it more time to stabilize. However, exponential backoff requires careful maximum timeout configuration—without a ceiling, timeouts could grow indefinitely. Typical implementations cap exponential backoff at reasonable values like 5 or 10 minutes. The tradeoff is responsiveness versus stability—longer timeouts provide more recovery time but delay traffic restoration.

## Request Volume-Based Thresholds
Some circuit breaker implementations use request volume as part of their threshold logic, requiring a minimum request count before considering failure percentages. For example, a circuit breaker might open only if there are 20 or more failures within 100 requests. This prevents flaky behavior when traffic is minimal—a single failure when receiving one request per minute shouldn't trigger the circuit breaker. Volume-based thresholds provide stability in low-traffic scenarios while maintaining responsiveness in high-traffic scenarios. Implementation requires tracking both successful and failed requests over a time window. The minimum volume threshold must be set considering your service's normal request rates and acceptable detection latency.

## State Transition Logging and Metrics
Proper observability of circuit breaker state transitions is essential for understanding system behavior during failures. Log every transition between states (closed→open, open→half-open, half-open→closed) with timestamps and reason. Metrics should track the number of transitions, time spent in each state, and frequency of service recovery from open state. These metrics help identify patterns—services that repeatedly enter open state suggest chronic issues requiring architectural changes. Transition logs provide crucial debugging information during incidents, showing exactly when protection mechanisms engaged and for how long. Without proper logging, diagnosing circuit breaker behavior becomes nearly impossible. Include context like failure rates, specific error types, and downstream service names in transition events.

## Distributed System Consistency Challenges
In distributed systems with multiple instances, circuit breaker state consistency across instances becomes important. If instance A opens its circuit while instance B remains closed, traffic distribution becomes uneven. Some implementations share circuit breaker state via a distributed store like Redis or ZooKeeper. This ensures all instances see consistent state and coordinate responses. However, shared state adds latency and introduces new failure modes—if the state store becomes unavailable, instances must decide whether to open preemptively or continue normally. Alternative approaches use local circuit breakers with careful timeout coordination, accepting temporary inconsistency. The choice depends on acceptable consistency window and tolerance for temporary uneven load distribution.

## Distinguishing Failure Types
Different failure types may warrant different circuit breaker responses. Network timeouts, resource exhaustion errors (429/503), and application errors (500) might have different severity levels. Some implementations track failure type separately, potentially using different thresholds for different error categories. For example, timeouts might require 10 occurrences to open the circuit while 503s might require only 3. This requires categorizing failures when recording them. The tradeoff is configuration complexity versus precision—more fine-grained failure classification enables better protection but requires more careful configuration and maintenance.

## Health Check Integration
Circuit breakers can integrate with health check endpoints to make more informed state transition decisions. Rather than relying solely on request failures, a half-open circuit breaker might call an explicit health endpoint. A successful health check indicates recovery; a failure confirms the service remains unhealthy. This provides more direct evidence of service status independent of actual traffic. However, health endpoints add dependencies and require maintenance—if the health endpoint lies about service status, the circuit breaker makes wrong decisions. Also, health endpoint unavailability doesn't necessarily mean the service is unhealthy. Health check integration works best when combined with request-based metrics rather than replacing them entirely.

## Consumer vs Producer Circuit Breakers
Circuit breakers can be implemented on the caller side (consumer) or receiver side (producer). Consumer-side circuit breakers protect the caller from calling unhealthy services. Producer-side circuit breakers protect the service from being overwhelmed by requests. Consumer-side breakers provide faster local protection but must exist at each caller. Producer-side breakers operate centrally but require communication to enforce. Most implementations use consumer-side circuit breakers because they provide local control and don't require receiver changes. Producer-side mechanisms are better suited to rate limiting. Using both together provides defense at multiple levels.

## Hybrid Timeouts and Circuit Breaker Strategy
Combining timeout strategies with circuit breaker patterns creates robust protection against various failure modes. A short per-request timeout (e.g., 5 seconds) catches slow responses quickly. A circuit breaker with lower thresholds (e.g., 20% failure rate) catches patterns of failures across multiple requests. Together, these ensure failed requests return control quickly rather than holding resources. The circuit breaker timeout (time in open state) should be longer than the request timeout to give the service recovery time. For example: 5-second request timeout, circuit opens after 20% failures, 30-second open timeout. This layered approach balances responsiveness with stability.

## Graceful Degradation Planning
Effective circuit breaker implementation requires thoughtful degradation strategies for when circuits open. Some operations simply cannot degrade—you cannot approve payment without actually processing it. However, many operations can serve older cached data, simplified responses, or skip optional operations. Plan degradation paths during architecture design, not during incidents. Document which services are essential versus optional and what each service should do when its dependency opens. Include feature flags that can disable non-essential functionality when circuits open. Test degradation paths regularly under failure conditions. This planning transforms circuit breaker openings from alarming failures into controlled degradation with maintained service availability.

## Recovery Validation Strategies
When a circuit breaker considers transitioning from open state back to closed, some implementations perform additional validation beyond the single half-open test request. Validation strategies might require multiple successful requests during half-open state, or delayed transitions that wait additional time after successful probes. These provide confidence that recovery is genuine rather than temporary. The tradeoff is slower recovery (delaying traffic restoration) versus false positives (reopening circuit immediately after closing). The most reliable validation combines successful half-open probes with health metrics from the downstream service, providing multiple signals of recovery.

## Monitoring and Alerting for Circuit State
Comprehensive monitoring enables early detection of circuit breaker issues before they cascade. Create alerts for circuits opening frequently, spending too long in open state, or alternating between open and closed states rapidly. Alert thresholds should be tuned to your service's characteristics—if a circuit opens once per month, that's normal; if it opens multiple times per hour, something requires investigation. Dashboard visibility into circuit breaker state across all dependencies helps identify patterns across your system. Include metadata about which services are critical versus secondary in your monitoring—a secondary service opening is less urgent than a primary one. Export circuit breaker metrics to your monitoring system to maintain historical visibility into system health patterns.
